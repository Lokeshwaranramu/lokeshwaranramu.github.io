How it works
Interest is charged dailyon (loan balance − offset balance) × rate ÷ 365. Money parked even for a few days between pays reduces interest.
Offset vs redrawBoth cut interest the same way, but offset money is your money in your account — instantly accessible. Redraw sits inside the loan; banks can restrict or absorb it, and redrawing from an investment-linked loan has tax consequences.
Offset vs savings accountSavings interest is taxed at your marginal rate; offset "returns" aren't taxed at all. That's why the equivalent-rate figure above is so high.
Managing it — the routine that works
1 · Salary lands hereHave your full pay deposited into the offset, not a separate transaction account. Every day it sits there counts.
2 · Emergency fund lives hereKeep 3–6 months of expenses in the offset instead of a savings account — same safety net, better return, zero tax.
3 · Delay money leavingPay monthly bills as late as their due date, or put spending on a credit card with an interest-free period and sweep it in full each month — cash stays offsetting longer. Only do this if you always pay the card in full.
4 · Check it's 100% offsetSome basic loans offer partial offset or none. Package loans with 100% offset often carry a ~$250–$400/yr fee — worth it once your average balance clears roughly $5,000–$7,000 at today's rates.
5 · Don't let it leakIts weakness is accessibility. Set a floor (e.g. never below your emergency fund) and treat anything above it as untouchable loan money. If temptation wins, extra repayments beat offset.
For your setup: pay both the $10k buffer and each fortnight's salary into the offset, run the household from it, and let the balance ratchet up. Combined with unchanged repayments after any rate cut, this is how single-income families quietly knock 5–8 years off a loan.
